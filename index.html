Teaching Philosophy

Imagine a classroom where students wrestle with real questions connected to their lives ‚Äî where understanding is built slowly, through inquiry, and where every learner has the agency to think, reason, and make their thinking visible. That's what I'm trying to build, anyway. I don't always get it right, and some lessons land better in my head than in the room. But the intent is consistent: active learning that puts students in the driver's seat. Not the polished, Pinterest-worthy kind ‚Äî the messy, productive kind where they investigate real-world problems, challenge assumptions, and can explain not just what they know but how they got there. Whether we're mapping Cold War proxy conflicts or debating whether Bitcoin can replace fiat currency, the goal is the same ‚Äî think harder, think visibly, and connect what happens in the classroom to the world outside it.

My stance on AI in education didn't come easily. It started with an internal crisis ‚Äî a genuine fear that AI would erode the very learning I was trying to build, that it would hand students a superficial sheen of competence without any of the struggle that makes knowledge stick. But through trial and error ‚Äî experimenting, failing, rethinking ‚Äî I came to see AI literacy not as an optional add-on but as a cornerstone of modern education. Teaching students to use AI critically, ethically, and reflectively is harder than ignoring it. It demands more of us as educators. But the gains are significant, and the cost of looking away is an abdication of our responsibility to prepare students for the world they're actually entering.

Everything on this site ‚Äî the interactive lessons, the resources, the experiments that worked and the ones still in progress ‚Äî is here because I believe in practising what I preach. If I ask my students to make their thinking visible, I should do the same. This is my learning, made visible.

General

Individuals & Societies is assessed across four criteria: A (Knowing and Understanding), B (Investigating), C (Communicating), and D (Thinking Critically). Each criterion is marked on a 1‚Äì8 scale using IB-published descriptors.

ICT / Design runs on an ipsative assessment model ‚Äî student performance is assessed against the student's own progress rather than a fixed external standard. This means growth, effort, and improvement over time are what matter most, not comparison to peers.

If you're unsure what a summative assessment is asking you to do, look at the command terms ‚Äî they tell you exactly what kind of thinking is expected.

IB command terms tell you what kind of response is expected. Here are the most common ones you'll see in my classes:

Describe ‚Äî Give a detailed account or picture of a situation. Explain ‚Äî Give a detailed account including reasons or causes. Analyse ‚Äî Break down to bring out the essential elements. Evaluate ‚Äî Make an appraisal by weighing up strengths and limitations. Discuss ‚Äî Offer a considered and balanced review. To what extent ‚Äî Consider the merits or otherwise of an argument.
